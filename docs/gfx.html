Technical Deep Dive

The urban simulator genre presents unique challenges: high entity counts and long draw distances. Below is the detailed analysis of why specific techniques were selected for the `urban_sprawl` codebase.

Instanced Rendering

Priority: Critical

Problem: Submitting 10,000 distinct `draw()` calls for buildings chokes the CPU driver overhead.

Solution: Group identical meshes (e.g., "Building_Type_A"). Send one draw call with a buffer containing transform matrices for all 500 instances.

Implementation: Medium +400% FPS

Texture Arrays

Priority: High

Problem: Cities have diverse textures. Switching textures breaks batching/instancing.

Solution: Load all building textures into a `Texture2DArray`. Pass a texture index in the instance data. Allows rendering *different* looking buildings in the *same* draw call.

Implementation: Hard Enables Batching

Cascaded Shadow Maps

Realism: High

Problem: Standard shadow maps look pixelated for huge city scales.

Solution: Render shadows into 3-4 "cascades" (layers). High res for close buildings, low res for distant skyscrapers. Essential for "Golden Hour" city shots.

Implementation: Hard -15% FPS

Screen Space AO (SSAO)

Realism: Medium

Problem: City streets look flat; geometry doesn't feel grounded.

Solution: A post-process pass that calculates occlusion based on the depth buffer. Adds soft shadows in corners/alleys, drastically improving depth perception.

Implementation: Easy -5% FPS

Impostors / LOD

Priority: Medium

Problem: Rendering full geometry for a house 2km away is wasteful.

Solution: Render distant buildings as simple cubes or even 2D billboards (impostors) facing the camera.

Implementation: Medium +50% FPS

Deferred Rendering

Arch Change

Problem: Forward rendering slows down with many street lights.

Solution: Separate geometry pass (G-Buffer) from lighting pass. Allows for hundreds of dynamic street lights with consistent performance.

Implementation: Very Hard Scale w/ Lights
